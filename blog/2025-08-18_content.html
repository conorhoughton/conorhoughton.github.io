Orange.

l

The word for orange in Arabic is burtuqāl; standard Arabic has no "p" sound and this word comes for Portugal because the Portugese were the first to bring oranges in large amounts to places people spoke Arabic!

I learned this recently because I was in Amsterdam and was surprised to discover Dutch has different words for orange the colour: oranje and orange the fruit: sinaasappel, from Chinese apple. I had thought all European languages had the same word, that none of them had words for the colour orange until the fruit arrived.

It turns out the story is much more complicated and in the usual glossomantic way, a little slice of our vast history. Our word, orange, is not derived as I have long believe, from the fruit we eat, the sweet orange. It is based instead on the Arabic word for the bitter orange, the related fruit that is used to make marmalade, orange oils and candied peels: it is too bitter to be eaten, as is, for pleasure. The cultivars and know how for growing the bitter orange tree spread along the silk road and arrived in Southern Europe around tenth century. This fruit had a name that had made the same journey as the tree, from Sanskrit, through Persian to Arabic.

  • Sanskrit: नारङ्ग — nāraṅga
  • Persian (historical): نارنگ — nārang
  • Arabic (medieval): نارنج — nāranj
This becamse Spanish naranja and ultimately orange. It lost the "n" to an article, you can imagine "a norange" getting mixed up with "an orange"; this is more-or-less what happened but it happened before the word reached English.

However, the use of orange as a word for a colour did begin until after the sweet orange arrived during the age of exploration, beginning in the fifteen century. Before that time journeys took too long to carry a fruit as perishable as an orange, bitter or sweet, across long distances; transportation was also expensive and more appropriate for things with high-value, spices and glazed pottery. This meant the bitter orange was unknown in the Netherlands so when Portuguese traders arrived in France with sweet oranges they were named after their bitter relatives, in the Netherlands, however, they were named after the place they came from sinaasappel.

The age of exploration was a time of vast change, of expansion and with that languages expanded to not just encompass words for new animals, new objects and new plants; new adjectives were also needed. With the popularity and prestige of the sweet orange came a new colour word: orange. This word started in France or England and spread, in countries where the sweet orange had a name like orange, it took the same word as for the fruit, in countries like the Netherlands, where the fruit had a completely different name, the colour was named, or invented, with a different word to the word for the fruit.

The surprising thing is that Portugese merchants were so closely associated with sweet oranges some languages named the fruit after Portugal: Armenian, Modern Standard Arabic and many other forms of Arabic, modern Persian, some Slavic languages, Greek, Georgian, Albanian and Turkish. In some of these languages the colour is named after the bitter orange, in Persian it is نارنجی — nāranjī and in Turkish turuncu; the Turkish word for bitter orange, turunç, comes from the same Sanskrit source as orange but has been on a longer phonetic journey. The others, MSA and so on, all name the colour after the sweet orange.
